States 4 & 8 Are Clock Artifacts, Not Market Structure

64% of state 4 (bear pause) and state 8 (bull pause) occurrences are EARLY ticks — ticks that arrived before the 0.5s clock fired. Every single one of these has zero price movement. The "flat" in states 4 and 8 is not price consolidation: it's the feed repeating the last-known price while waiting for the next clock emission. Their near-zero directional edge (~0.2–0.5%) is explained entirely by this mechanical artifact. If you were building a strategy around these states, you'd be trading feed latency noise, not market information.

Early Ticks Are Near-Perfect Predictors for Directional States

When a tick arrives early AND it forms a directional state, the predictive edge amplifies dramatically toward certainty. State 2 (bull staircase): +57.1% → +98.1%. State 6 (bear staircase): −57.1% → −98.1%. State 7 (bull reversal): +88.6% → +99.5%. State 11 (bear reversal): −88.4% → −99.6%. The mechanism: an early tick means the price moved urgently — before the regular clock window. This urgency is a signal that the move has conviction. On clock ticks, there's always some probability the move was just the clock's random update. On early ticks, the move happened out-of-schedule, which is much less common and more informative.

Early → Late is the Dominant Transition (54.4%)

After an early tick, more than half the time the next tick is late. This is a clock compensation effect: when a tick fires early, it shifts the effective clock position forward, causing the next emission to fall later in relative time. This creates a predictable oscillation in gap sizes. Strategically: if you observe an early tick, the next tick's gap will be late (>0.52s) more often than not. This has implications for time-based strategies — the inter-tick timing is not memoryless (gap autocorrelation at lag-3 = −0.107, lag-5 = −0.177).

The 0.30–0.44s Dead Zone: 96–99.9% Price Repeats

Ticks arriving 300–440ms after the previous tick carry almost zero price information — they are overwhelmingly price repeats. This is the "dead zone" between two consecutive clock beats. A tick in this range means: the previous tick fired at time T, and a new tick arrived at T+0.35s for example, too far from T to still carry the same clock's price, but too early for the next 0.5s clock to have generated a new price. The result is a repeated last price. Compare: 0.30–0.35s = 96.4% zeros; 0.35–0.40s = 99.9% zeros; 0.40–0.44s = 99.7% zeros.

The 0.51–0.52s Double-Fire Zone: 3× Volatility

Ticks arriving 510–520ms after the previous tick have a mean price move of 0.00520 — more than 3× the surrounding gap bands (~0.0016). This is the "double-fire" phenomenon: a clock tick fired at the regular ~0.5s mark, AND a quote revision arrived within ~10–20ms. The two events are compressed into a single observed tick that appears "late" by 10–20ms but actually contains two price updates stacked together. These rare ticks (~32k occurrences, 8% of normal-gap ticks) are the highest-volatility moments in the data.

Rock-Solid Clock: 2.07–2.09 TPS Across All 14 Sessions

Across 14 separate trading sessions spanning over 100 hours, the tick rate never deviates meaningfully from 2.08 ticks/second. The mean gap across sessions ranges only from 0.4794s to 0.4845s — a total spread of 5.1ms. This rules out adaptive throttling, congestion-based slowdown, or any session-level variation in the feed's emission rate. The feed is a fixed-cadence synthetic clock. Implications for the Markov analysis: the state system is driven primarily by a deterministic timer with Gaussian jitter, not by organic order flow dynamics. The "edge" in the states comes from price update patterns around this timer, not from exchange microstructure.
